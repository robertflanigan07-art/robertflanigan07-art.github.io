Space travel has always fascinated humanity, and since the Space Race between the Soviet Union and the United States, space technology has remained in the public eye. In our modern age, the most prevalent problem facing space travel is the rocket equation:

\( \Delta v = v_e \ln \frac{m_0}{m_f} \)

In this equation \( \Delta v \) is a change in velocity, \( v_e \) is exhaust velocity, \( m_0 \) is the initial, or wet mass of a spacecraft, and \( m_f \) is the final, or dry mass of the craft. This equation allows us to conclude that to increase \( \Delta v \), one must either increase exhaust velocity, increase the amount of fuel carried, or decrease the dry mass of the spacecraft. However, increasing the fuel carried or decreasing the dry mass of the spacecraft is not ideal because \( \frac{m_0}{m_f} \) is the argument to the natural logarithm. This means that increasing \( \frac{m_0}{m_f} \) has diminishing returns when trying to increase \( \Delta v \). The best way to increase the amount of \( \Delta v \) a spacecraft can carry is to increase the exhaust velocity.
